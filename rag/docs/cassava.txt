CASSAVA CROP ADVISORY GUIDE
Source: FAO Agronomy Guidelines for Cassava Production

OVERVIEW
Cassava (Manihot esculenta) is a drought-tolerant root crop and a primary calorie source for over 800 million people in tropical Africa, Asia, and Latin America. It is one of the most resilient crops under poor soil and dry conditions.

OPTIMAL GROWING CONDITIONS
- Temperature: 25-29°C
- Rainfall: 1000-1500 mm per year; tolerates 600 mm minimum
- Soil pH: 5.5 to 7.0
- Soil Type: Well-drained, light to medium-textured soils (sandy loam ideal)
- Cassava is one of the few crops that produces well in poor, acidic soils

PLANTING
- Planting Material: Stem cuttings (stakes) 20-30 cm long from healthy plants
- Planting Method: Vertical, horizontal, or slanted — slanted preferred for dry areas
- Spacing: 1m x 1m (10,000 plants/ha) for fresh market; 1m x 0.8m for starch
- Planting Season: Beginning of rainy season for rain-fed; any time with irrigation

FERTILIZER REQUIREMENTS
- Cassava is known for growing in poor soils but responds well to fertilizers
- Nitrogen (N): 60-80 kg/ha
- Phosphorus (P): 40-60 kg/ha
- Potassium (K): 80-100 kg/ha (most critical — reduces cyanide content)
- Organic manure: 5-10 tonnes/ha improves structure and yield
- Avoid excess nitrogen in early stages — promotes vegetative growth over roots

WATER MANAGEMENT
- Cassava is drought-tolerant but not drought-immune
- Critical water period: first 3 months after planting (root establishment)
- Waterlogging causes root rot — ensure proper drainage
- Water stress after 6 months affects starch accumulation

PEST AND DISEASE MANAGEMENT
- Major Pests: Cassava Mealybug, Cassava Green Mite, Whitefly
- Cassava Mealybug: Biological control with Anagyrus lopezi (parasitoid wasp) — very effective
- Whitefly vectors CMD (Cassava Mosaic Disease) — remove infected plants immediately
- Major Diseases: Cassava Mosaic Disease (CMD), Cassava Brown Streak Disease (CBSD)
- Use virus-indexed, clean planting material — most important intervention
- Cassava Bacterial Blight: Copper-based bactericide; avoid working in wet fields
- Pesticide overuse damages beneficial insects and increases pest resistance

YIELD BENCHMARKS
- Low yield: Below 8 t/ha — disease-infected planting material, poor soil, drought
- Average yield: 8 to 15 t/ha — typical smallholder farming in Africa/Asia
- Good yield: 15 to 25 t/ha — improved varieties with good management
- Excellent yield: Above 25 t/ha — high-input commercial farming

CYANIDE CONTENT
- All cassava contains cyanogenic glucosides (HCN)
- Sweet varieties: < 50 mg HCN/kg fresh weight (safe after cooking)
- Bitter varieties: > 200 mg HCN/kg (must be processed before consumption)
- Proper soaking, drying, or fermentation removes cyanide
- Never eat raw bitter cassava — it is toxic

POST-HARVEST
- Harvest 8-24 months after planting depending on variety and use
- Fresh roots deteriorate within 2-3 days — process or sell quickly
- Dry chips or flour for longer shelf life (12+ months)
- Store processed products in moisture-proof bags

SAFETY AND DISCLAIMER
Raw bitter cassava contains toxic levels of hydrocyanic acid (HCN). Always process before consumption. Use certified disease-free planting material to prevent CMD and CBSD spread. Pesticide applications must follow label directions. Consult local extension for variety selection suited to your region.
